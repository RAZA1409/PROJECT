Black Rhino (Diceros bicornis)

The Black Rhino is critically endangered, known for its two horns on the skull and a hooked upper lip. It is native to the eastern and southern parts of Africa, including Kenya, Tanzania, Namibia, and Zimbabwe.

Physical Characteristics

Black Rhinos are smaller than their white rhino cousins, weighing between 1,800 to 3,100 pounds (800 to 1,400 kg). They have a pointed prehensile upper lip that helps them grasp leaves and branches from shrubs and trees.

Habitat and Diet

Black Rhinos inhabit grasslands, savannas, and tropical bushlands. Their diet consists of leafy plants, branches, shoots, thorny bushes, and fruit. They are browsers rather than grazers.

Conservation Status

Once widespread across sub-Saharan Africa, Black Rhino numbers have dramatically decreased due to poaching for their horns. Despite conservation efforts, fewer than 5,500 remain in the wild today, classifying them as "Critically Endangered."
